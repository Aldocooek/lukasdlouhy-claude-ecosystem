## 1. Direct-Response Copywriting Laws

### Claude Hopkins (Scientific Advertising, 1923)
The laws that have never been overturned:

1. **Offer service, not cleverness.** The advertiser is a salesman. The copy is a conversation with one person. Every word that doesn't sell must be cut.
2. **Headlines are the ad.** If the headline fails, nothing else matters. On average, the headline is read by 5x more people than the body (Ogilvy confirmed this, 1963).
3. **Specificity beats superlatives.** "Removes 98.3% of bacteria in 30 seconds" > "Kills more germs than any other cleaner."
4. **Curiosity is the hook; specificity is the close.** Open loops compel attention. Specifics build credibility.
5. **Always test.** Judgment is inferior to data. Split test everything — headline, offer, CTA — before scaling.
6. **Reason-why copy outperforms mere assertion.** Explain WHY the price is low, WHY the product works, WHY the offer expires. Reason increases believability.
7. **The coupon (CTA) measures everything.** If you can't measure response, you're doing brand advertising, not direct response.

### David Ogilvy (Confessions of an Advertising Man, 1963)
- Headline is read by 5x more people than body copy — spend 80% of time on it.
- On average, news value increases recall by 22%. Make the headline newsworthy.
- Long copy outperforms short copy for high-ticket products. Interested readers read everything.
- Every word in the body must earn its place. Each sentence must pull the reader into the next.
- Never use adjectives without proof: "best," "superior," "unique" are forbidden without substantiation.
- Photographs of people in use outperform product-only shots.
- The strongest word in advertising: FREE (when genuine). Second: NEW.

### Joe Sugarman (Advertising Secrets of the Written Word)
- **Slippery Slide:** The sole purpose of every sentence is to get the reader to read the next sentence. Create an irresistible downward pull. Headline → subheadline → first sentence → second sentence... until the offer.
- **Buying decision is emotional; justification is rational.** Sell the emotion, provide the justification. The reader has already decided with System 1 — give System 2 the data to approve it.
- **Every objection is an opportunity.** Raise and handle objections in the copy before the reader formulates them.
- **Copy length = length of purchase decision.** Impulse buy: short copy. High-ticket B2B: long. Never cut copy that sells.
- **The PS is the second most-read element.** Restate offer + scarcity + one key benefit.

### Gary Halbert (The Boron Letters + Halbert Newsletter)
- **Starving crowd > everything.** Market selection (40%) + audience targeting (40%) > copy quality (20%). A hungry crowd with a mediocre offer beats a genius copy writer with the wrong product to the wrong list.
- **Specificity = credibility.** "1,247,384 customers served" > "over a million customers." The specific number reads as measured; the round number reads as invented.
- **Bullets promise outcomes, never reveal the mechanism.** "The 3-word phrase that makes prospects say yes before hearing the pitch" — mechanism left out. Gap stays open. Reader must buy to close it.
- **Rule of One:** One reader, one big idea, one dominant emotion, one CTA. Every element serves one purpose.
- **Fascinations library:** Build 100 bullet points for any product and you will always have 5-10 brilliant ones. Quantity generates quality.

### Alex Hormozi ($100M Offers — Value Equation)
```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Probability of Achievement)
        ÷ (Time Delay × Effort and Sacrifice)
```
To increase perceived value:
- Increase Dream Outcome size (concrete, ambitious, measurable)
- Increase Perceived Probability (proof, case studies, guarantees, specificity)
- Decrease Time Delay (speed of results — "in 30 days," "in the first session")
- Decrease Effort and Sacrifice (ease, done-for-you, templates, automation)

Hormozi's offer construction hierarchy:
1. Identify the dream outcome (what they really want, not what they say)
2. Identify all obstacles between now and outcome
3. Convert each obstacle into a deliverable/service
4. Name every deliverable and assign it a value
5. Stack bonuses adjacent to core offer — same category, complementary
6. Add risk reversal (guarantee) last — removes final objection

### Russell Brunson (Expert Secrets — Hook Framework)
- **Hook → Story → Offer** is the fundamental unit of persuasion.
- Hook: stops the scroll, creates a pattern interrupt, delivers a promise
- Story: creates belief change — from current false belief to new enabling belief
- Offer: presents the vehicle for achieving the transformation
- **Epiphany Bridge:** The story that explains how you discovered the big idea. First-person transformation. "Before I was X, then I discovered Y, now I'm Z."
- **3 Core Stories for any product:**
  1. Origin story (why you built this — Epiphany Bridge)
  2. Internal belief story (why they can do it)
  3. External belief story (why this specific vehicle/product works)

## 2. Hook Frameworks — 15 Named Patterns

### Principle: 0-3 seconds is the make-or-break window
- Meta data: ads that hold attention past 3 seconds have 60% higher chance of driving meaningful action
- TikTok: hook retention at 1.5s determines whether the algorithm distributes or buries
- Instagram Reels: first 3 frames (≈1.5s at 24fps) must create enough curiosity to stop the thumb
- 85% of Meta video is watched with sound off → visual hook must function without audio; text overlay on frame 1 is mandatory
- Target: 50%+ scroll-stopping rate (hook-to-view ratio); below 20% = kill creative

### 1. Curiosity Gap
Named and formalized by George Loewenstein (Carnegie Mellon). Gap between what you know and what you want to know. Compulsion to close the gap drives attention.

Pattern: "The [specific thing] most [audience] never figure out"
Example: "The one Meta setting most e-commerce brands miss — and it's costing them 30% of their ROAS"
Example: "What a $47,000/day ad account does differently in the first 3 seconds"
Rule: Name the payoff category, withhold the mechanism. Never resolve the gap in the hook.

### 2. Pattern Interrupt
Violates genre expectations. The viewer's visual cortex is calibrated for patterns — disruption forces conscious attention. (Neuroscience: salient unexpected stimuli trigger orienting reflex via superior colliculus.)

Pattern: [Something unexpected / visually wrong / counterintuitive]
Example (visual): White text on black frame surrounded by colorful feed content
Example (audio): Silence or whisper when all competitors are loud
Example (text): "Stop. This is not the ad you think it is."
Rule: The interrupt must be followed by a payoff that justifies the disruption. An interrupt with no relevant follow-through = high skip rate.

### 3. Contrarian Claim
Takes the opposite position of conventional wisdom. Activates cognitive dissonance — must resolve.

Pattern: "Why [widely accepted belief] is actually [wrong / costing you / a myth]"
Example: "Why posting more on Instagram is killing your reach"
Example: "Stop split-testing your ad copy. Here's what actually moves ROAS."
Rule: Must be defensible with data. False contrarianism that gets debunked in the body kills credibility.

### 4. Problem-Aware Hook
For audiences who know they have the problem but don't know the solution. Meets them where they are.

Pattern: "Still [suffering from problem]? Here's what's actually causing it."
Example: "Still getting high CPMs on Meta despite good creative? This is why."
Example: "Your Facebook ads aren't converting. The reason is almost never the ad."
Rule: The problem must be specific. "Struggling with ads" → too broad. "CPMs above €18 on cold traffic" → target-specific.

### 5. Problem-Unaware Hook
For audiences who don't know they have a problem. Must first create awareness.

Pattern: "You're [doing common thing] and it's [causing hidden damage]"
Example: "Every time you increase a Meta budget by more than 30%, you reset the algorithm. Most brands do this wrong."
Example: "Your TikTok ads are reaching the wrong people and here's how to tell."
Rule: Frame as discovery, not accusation. "You might not know..." or "Most brands don't realize..." softens the entry.

### 6. Contextual (Situational)
References a specific situation the viewer is in right now. Creates "this ad knows me" response.

Pattern: "If you're [running X] right now, [this matters]"
Example: "If you're scaling a Meta campaign to over €10K/day, this is the one thing to check first."
Example: "Running Google PMax? Your brand terms might be cannibalizing search."
Rule: The more specific the context, the smaller the audience but the higher the resonance. Use narrow context for retargeting; broader context for cold.

### 7. Social Proof Hook
Opens with the proof. Credibility first, explanation second.

Pattern: "[Specific metric] — here's how we did it"
Example: "4.8x ROAS. 30 days. €0 to €45K ad spend. Here's the exact structure."
Example: "174 investors. Zero defaults since 2019. This is why they chose us over banks."
Rule: Use the most impressive metric that can be substantiated. Social proof without verifiability = trust liability.

### 8. Urgency Hook
Time or quantity constraint creates pressure to act now vs. "later" (which is never).

Pattern: "[Deadline / limit] + [what they lose / miss]"
Example: "This offer closes Friday. After that, the price doubles."
Example: "We have 12 remaining spots for Q2 onboarding. Application takes 4 minutes."
Rule: The urgency must be real. Fake countdown timers were effective in 2015; 2026 audiences detect them instantly and distrust the brand. Use real scarcity (cohort size, production capacity, actual deadline).

### 9. Status-Elevation Hook
Flatters the viewer's self-concept or their aspirational identity. Activates mimetic desire.

Pattern: "What [high-status group] does that [average person] ignores"
Example: "What top 1% e-commerce brands do with their Meta creative that others copy 6 months later."
Example: "The way that €50M/year businesses think about paid acquisition is completely different."
Rule: The viewer must see themselves as capable of joining the high-status group. Aspirational but not delusional.

### 10. Transformation Hook (Before-After)
Shows the state transition. Emotional resonance of the journey.

Pattern: "[Before state] → [After state] — [how long / how hard]"
Example: "I was spending €15K/month on Meta with 1.2x ROAS. Six weeks later: 4.1x ROAS, half the budget."
Example: "Couldn't close a single investor meeting. Then changed one sentence in the email. 3 meetings booked in a week."
Rule: The before state must be recognizable. The after state must be specific. Generic transformation ("my life changed") is worthless.

### 11. Pain Amplification Hook
Focuses on the pain, makes it bigger, more specific, more immediate.

Pattern: "[Specific bad thing] is happening right now and you might not see it yet"
Example: "Your ad fatigue is setting in. Your CPMs are climbing. Your ROAS is falling. And if you don't change the creative this week, your campaign is about to stall."
Example: "Every day you run the same ad creative, frequency rises. After frequency 3.5, performance drops 40%. Check yours."
Rule: Must be true and actionable. Pure pain without a solution exit creates anxiety, not conversion. Always lead out with the path forward.

### 12. Vivid Imagery Hook
Creates a sensory-rich mental picture. Activates the dorsal visual pathway; imagery processing precedes language comprehension.

Pattern: [Describe a scene the viewer can picture and place themselves in]
Example: "You're looking at your dashboard. The ROAS line is going down. The budget counter is going up. Your best ad stopped working and you don't know which one to kill first."
Example: "Imagine opening your Stripe notifications on a Monday morning and seeing 47 new sales — from a campaign you set up on Friday."
Rule: Make the scene feel real and immediate. Second-person ("you") is stronger than third-person ("they").

### 13. Surprising Statistic Hook
Opens with a number that violates expectation.

Pattern: "[Counterintuitive number] + brief contextualization"
Example: "85% of Meta video is watched on mute. Most ads are built for audio."
Example: "3 seconds. That's how long before 60% of viewers skip pre-roll YouTube. Your hook has 3 seconds."
Rule: The stat must be surprising relative to the viewer's assumption. If they already know it, it's not a hook — it's a headline. Source the stat or it reads as fabricated.

### 14. Contrarian Confession
Admits something most brands/experts won't say. Radical honesty = instant trust.

Pattern: "I have to tell you something about [common practice] that most [experts] won't admit"
Example: "Broad targeting on Meta is almost always better than interest stacking. I used to sell interest-based setups. I was wrong."
Example: "Most A/B tests you run on your ads are statistically useless. Here's why and what to do instead."
Rule: The confession must be genuine. A fake vulnerability designed to sell is detected immediately.

### 15. If-Then Conditional
Identifies the viewer through a conditional statement they will accept or reject.

Pattern: "If you [do X] / [are X type], then [this matters to you]"
Example: "If you're spending more than €5K/month on paid ads and your ROAS is below 2.5x, this is for you."
Example: "If you're a founder who's tried Meta ads and got burned, you need to hear this before you touch it again."
Rule: The "if" qualifier must be specific enough to select for the right audience. Broad "if" = low resonance. Tight "if" = high resonance with smaller but better-qualified audience.

## 3. Copy Formulas — 8 with Filled Examples

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve (Gary Halbert / Dan Kennedy)
Most used in: social ads primary text, email, landing page sections

```
PROBLEM: Your Meta ROAS dropped below 2x and you don't know why.
AGITATE:  Every day you run the same creative, frequency climbs, CPMs rise,
          and your algorithm is learning from the wrong signals. The longer
          you wait, the harder it gets to recover — because the machine
          has already burned through your best audiences.
SOLVE:    The Creative Rotation System gives you 3 new angles per week,
          kills losers by day 4, and keeps your ROAS stable above 3.5x.
          [CTA: See how it works]
```

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action (E. St. Elmo Lewis, 1898)
Most used in: full-page ads, email sequences, landing pages

```
ATTENTION: 4.8x ROAS in 30 days — without hiring an agency.
INTEREST:  Most e-commerce brands lose money on paid ads for the first
           3 months because they're testing the wrong thing first.
           The winning approach: test angles before copy; test copy
           before budget.
DESIRE:    Our 12-week media buying accelerator has produced 8 brands
           achieving 3x+ ROAS on cold traffic in the first month.
           Average CAC dropped 34%.
ACTION:    Apply for the next cohort — 8 spots only, Q3 opens Monday.
```

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge (Regis McKenna)
Most used in: social proof ads, testimonials, email subject lines

```
BEFORE: Running Google PMax with one asset group and wondering why
        it's burning through budget on irrelevant traffic.
AFTER:  Six months later: three separated asset groups by product
        category, brand excluded from PMax, shopping 40% more
        efficient, CPL down 28%.
BRIDGE: The structure change that made the difference took 45 minutes.
        Here's exactly what to do. [CTA]
```

### 4Ps — Picture, Promise, Prove, Push (Henry Hoke Sr.)
Most used in: direct mail, landing pages, video scripts

```
PICTURE:  You open your ads manager on Monday. Not to check what
          went wrong — to see how much you made over the weekend
          while you were offline.
PROMISE:  A properly structured Meta campaign runs itself once it's
          out of the learning phase. We show you how to get there
          and stay there.
PROVE:    Fourteen brands in this program hit positive ROAS within
          the first 30 days. Eight of those scaled past €50K/month
          in under 90 days.
PUSH:     The next cohort starts in 11 days. [Get your spot]
```

### Star-Story-Solution (Dan Kennedy)
Most used in: VSLs, email sequences, brand story ads

```
STAR:     In 2022, a Czech e-commerce founder was spending €8,000/month
          on Meta ads. His ROAS was 0.8x. He was losing money on every order.
STORY:    He tried everything: new creative, new audiences, new agencies.
          Nothing worked. Then he noticed: his best-selling product had
          one ad angle that never got tested — the problem-aware hook.
          He rebuilt the entire campaign around that one angle.
SOLUTION: In week 3, ROAS hit 2.9x. Week 6: 4.2x. Month 3: €45K/month
          spend at 3.8x sustained ROAS. The angle that saved his business
          is one of the 15 frameworks in this program.
```

### Future-Pacing (Brunson / NLP origin)
Most used in: VSL closes, high-ticket sales calls, email nurture

```
Imagine it's 90 days from now.

You open your laptop on a Tuesday morning. Your dashboard shows
three campaigns — all green. Your automated creative refresh
launched two new ads yesterday; one is already outperforming the
control by 22%. Your CPA target was €18. You're running at €11.40.

You have more budget than you know what to do with, because the
algorithm is working with you, not against you.

That version of you isn't very far away. But it requires one
decision today. [CTA]
```

### Reason-Why (Claude Hopkins)
Most used in: price discount ads, limited offers, urgency ads

```
Why is this the last week we're running this offer?

Three reasons:

1. Our Q2 cohort closes at 20 clients. We're at 18. Two spots
   is not a business decision — it's a capacity constraint.
   We cannot effectively coach more than 20 brands simultaneously.

2. The onboarding process takes 14 days. Anyone joining after
   Friday won't finish before Q3 campaigns need to launch.

3. We're raising the price in Q3. The program cost €2,400 in Q1.
   It was €2,800 in Q2. Q3 price is €3,400.

This is the reason. [Reserve your spot]
```

### Slippery Slide (Joe Sugarman)
Most used in: long-form ad copy, email body, advertorials

```
Let me show you something.

Last month, we ran an experiment.

Same product. Same audience. Same budget — €10,000 on Meta.

The only thing we changed was the first three seconds of the video.

Version A: Product shot with logo reveal.
Version B: A founder sitting in his car, speaking directly to camera,
saying: "I need to tell you something about how most brands run
Facebook ads that nobody is saying out loud."

Version A spent €10,000. Got 12 sales. CPA: €833.

Version B spent €10,000. Got 94 sales. CPA: €106.

Same product. Same audience. Same budget.

Three seconds changed everything.

Here's exactly what Version B's hook did — and how to build it.
[Continue]
```

## 4. 8 Motivation Drivers

Based on Maslow's hierarchy mapped to commercial advertising triggers. (Industry-standard mapping; original formulation by Abraham Maslow, 1943.)

### 1. Fear (Threat-Avoidance)
Survival instinct. Most powerful driver when the threat is specific, credible, and proximate.
Ad application: "You're losing €X every month you don't fix this." Loss-framed copy.
Caution: Fear without an exit (the solution) creates paralysis, not action. Always pair with an achievable resolution.

### 2. Greed (Gain-Seeking)
The desire for more — money, time, leverage. Works for financial, productivity, and growth products.
Ad application: ROI-first headlines. "4.8x ROAS in 30 days." Specific financial outcomes.
Caution: Greed claims require proof. Without numbers and social proof, they read as hype.

### 3. Status (Social Comparison)
Desire to be perceived as high-status, successful, or belonging to an elite group.
Ad application: "What top-performing brands do differently." Identity-based positioning.
Research: Veblen goods demonstrate status overrides price sensitivity — premium pricing can increase desire.

### 4. Belonging (Tribal Identity)
Desire to join or remain part of a community with shared values.
Ad application: "Join 12,400 founders who..." Community framing. "Built for [specific type of person]."
Research: Cialdini's social proof is the most documented influence principle — 6 principle hierarchy, Influence, 1984.

### 5. Curiosity (Information Gap)
The Zeigarnik Effect and Loewenstein's Information Gap Theory — incomplete information creates intrinsic motivation to resolve.
Ad application: Curiosity-gap hooks. Questions without immediate answers. Numbered lists without revealing the items.
Research: Loewenstein (1994) — curiosity intensity scales with proximity to the answer. Greater the "almost knowing," greater the drive.

### 6. Certainty (Risk Reduction)
Desire for predictability and safety. Particularly powerful for high-ticket or high-trust purchases.
Ad application: Guarantees, third-party validation, specific proof, testimonials with names and numbers.
Research: Loss aversion is 2x stronger than gain motivation (Kahneman & Tversky, Prospect Theory, 1979).

### 7. Novelty (Neophilia)
Dopamine response to new information, new products, new methods. Drives early adopters.
Ad application: "New method," "what's changed in [category] in 2026," first-to-know framing.
Caution: Novelty without credibility triggers skepticism. Pair "new" with a mechanism explanation.

### 8. Significance (Legacy / Impact)
Desire to matter, leave a mark, be remembered. Powerful for B2B, high-ticket, mission-driven products.
Ad application: "This changes how your business will operate in 5 years." Long-term framing. Builder/founder identity.
Note: Tony Robbins' 6 Human Needs model (Certainty, Variety, Significance, Love/Connection, Growth, Contribution) extends Maslow specifically for commerce. Hormozi's offer construction maps directly to these.

## 5. Cognitive Biases for Ads — Effect Sizes

### Anchoring Effect
First price seen determines reference frame for all subsequent prices.
Effect: In negotiation studies, a high anchor increases final price by 15-30% (Galinsky & Mussweiler, 2001).
Ad application: Show full value stack before revealing offer price. "Total value: €3,400. Today: €997."

### Scarcity (Loss Aversion)
Loss aversion multiplier: ~2x (losses hurt twice as much as equivalent gains feel good — Kahneman & Tversky, 1979).
Effect: Scarcity messaging increases conversion by 20-30% when genuine (Cialdini, Influence, 2009 edition).
Ad application: Limited quantity, limited time, real constraints. "12 spots remaining" > "limited availability."
Warning: Fake scarcity is detectable by 2026 audiences. Credibility loss from exposure exceeds short-term conversion gain.

### Social Proof
Effect: 70% of people trust reviews from strangers online (Nielsen, 2012 — industry standard reference).
In Czech B2B context: referrals perform 3-5x better than cold outreach (Filip Dopita ecosystem data, 2026).
Specificity multiplier: "12,400 brands" converts 40% better than "thousands of brands" (A/B test data, ConversionXL).
Ad application: Lead with the specific number. Name, photo, and specific result for testimonials.

### Loss Aversion
Kahneman & Tversky: ~2x magnitude. "You're losing €2,400/month without this" > "You could gain €2,400/month."
Rule of application: Frame benefits as preventing loss, not achieving gain. Especially effective in financial, SaaS, and legal categories.

### Reciprocity
Cialdini: gifts given before asking create obligation to give back (Influence, 1984).
Ad application: Free resource → lead capture. Free value in content → brand goodwill. Provide value before asking for purchase.
Magnitude: In restaurant studies, candy with bill increased tips 3-14%. Online: free resource lead magnets convert 2-4x over bare opt-in (industry standard).

### Authority
Effect: Milgram obedience studies — authority signals drive compliance even against self-interest (1963, institutional).
Ad application: Logos, credentials, certifications, media mentions, third-party validation. ČNB registration is Czech-market authority signal for financial products.
Asymmetry: Authority claims you make about yourself are 80% less credible than authority signals others attribute to you.

### Commitment-Consistency (Cialdini)
Once someone makes a small commitment (micro-commitment), they are more likely to make larger consistent commitments.
Effect: Foot-in-the-door studies — initial small ask increases larger-ask compliance by 40-75% (Freedman & Fraser, 1966).
Ad application: Lead with a low-friction CTA ("Download the guide," "Calculate your score") before the purchase ask. Retargeting sequences exploit this — the next step is always slightly bigger.

### Liking
People are more likely to be influenced by people they like (similarity, compliments, familiarity).
Effect: Tutor effectiveness increases 20-40% when students rate tutor as likable (industry meta-analysis).
Ad application: Authentic founder/founder face + story. Genuine voice (Filip Dopita's style: direct, no corporate veneer). User-generated creative feels "like someone they know."

### Mere Exposure Effect
Familiarity breeds preference. Repeated exposure to a stimulus increases liking (Zajonc, 1968).
Effect: 7+ impressions before conversion is the commonly cited B2B threshold. For ads: frequency 2-4 is sweet spot; above 5 for cold audiences triggers ad fatigue.
Ad application: Retargeting is the commercial application. Consistent visual identity across touchpoints amplifies the effect.

### Zeigarnik Effect
Incomplete tasks are better remembered than completed ones (Zeigarnik, 1927).
Ad application: Open loops in hooks and email subject lines. "The 3 secrets to... (continued in the ad)" compels clicks. Serial content (Part 1, Part 2) keeps audiences returning.

## 6. Czech Market Specifics

### Language and Register
- **Vykání is mandatory** in all formal B2B communication. "Vy," "Vám," "Vás" — capitalize throughout.
- Consumer ads: vykání unless brand persona explicitly youthful (under-25 target, casual social platform).
- Tykání in B2C for Gen Z-targeted brands is acceptable but must be consistent from first touchpoint.
- Filip Dopita voice: "Max 2 věty na myšlenku." Numbers replace adjectives.
- Forbidden words in Filip's brand voice: "revoluční," "inovativní," "synergický," "v dnešní době," "S pozdravom."
- Blocked AI-pattern phrases: "Je důležité poznamenat," "Furthermore," "It's worth mentioning."

### Czech B2B Investor Psychology (Filip Dopita data, OneFlow 2026)
- 5-7 touchpoints required before a decision in Czech B2B (vs. 3-5 in DE/AT/UK)
- Conservative preference: safety-first framing over high-returns framing
- Referrals perform 3-5x over cold outreach — structure referral acquisition as a system
- Personal contact required for deals >500K CZK
- WhatsApp reply rate 26%+ for warm contacts (benchmark from OneFlow, April 2026)
- LinkedIn response rate: 5-8% with personalized note

### Regulatory — Czech Advertising Law

#### ZoOR (Zákon o regulaci reklamy, Act 40/1995 Coll.)
- **§2 — Nepřípustná reklama (Forbidden advertising):** Reklama nesmí být klamavá (misleading), skrytá (disguised), srovnávací v nevoleném rozsahu (improper comparative), podprahová (subliminal).
- Comparative advertising is permitted only if: comparing goods/services for same need, objective comparison, not denigrating competitor, no unfair advantage from competitor's reputation.
- Health claims in ads: regulated separately under Act 378/2007 (pharmaceuticals) and EU 1169/2011 (food).
- Forbidden: "nejlepší," "zaručené," "100% bezpečné" — unless you can prove them.

#### ČNB (Czech National Bank) — Financial Product Rules
- Financial products targeting retail investors must include: risk warnings, regulatory status, fees disclosure.
- Investment ads must not promise specific returns without qualifying with risk.
- ECSP (EU 2020/1503) effective CZ from November 10, 2023: crowdfunding platforms over €5M/year require CNB license.
- Bond issuance under €1M/year or ≤149 qualified investors: prospectus exemption applies.
- Social proof for financial products: specific past performance requires disclaimer that past performance does not guarantee future results.

#### GDPR (Czech implementation)
- Retargeting pixel: must have cookie consent before firing on EU traffic.
- Lead capture: must document opt-in basis (legitimate interest or explicit consent).
- Email marketing: unsubscribe link mandatory in every communication.

### Optimal Timing (Czech market)
- Instagram: Tue-Thu, 12:00-13:00 or 19:00-21:00
- Email: Tue-Wed, 07:00-09:00
- LinkedIn: Mon-Wed morning (08:00-10:00 local)
- Avoid: Friday afternoon, Saturday morning

## 7. Hook-Stopping Research Data — The 0-3 Second Window

### Platform-Specific Critical Windows

#### Meta (Facebook + Instagram)
- **0-3 second rule:** If the hook does not create a reason to continue watching in the first 3 seconds, the viewer scrolls.
- Meta internal data: ads that generate 3-second video views have 60% higher probability of driving meaningful downstream action vs. those that don't.
- **3-second sound check:** Because 85% of Meta video is consumed with sound off, every ad must communicate the hook visually. Text overlay on frame 1 is non-negotiable.
- Retention cliffs (industry benchmark data):
  - 25% completion: the first real filter (quality signal)
  - 50% completion: strong positive signal; indicates message-market fit
  - 75% completion: high intent; candidate for offer close
  - 95% completion: warm retarget immediately
- **Frequency thresholds:**
  - 1-2x: under-exposed; audience may not recall
  - 2-4x: optimal range for cold traffic
  - 3.5x+: diminishing returns begin (AdEspresso data); CPM rises, CTR falls
  - 5x+ cold: kill or refresh creative; fatigue is fully set

#### TikTok
- **1.5-second rule:** TikTok's algorithm makes distribution decisions based on scroll-through rate at the 1.5-second mark.
- Hook at frame 1-45 (1.5 seconds at 30fps) determines whether content gets distributed to wider audiences or suppressed.
- Completion rate is the primary quality signal. Re-watches are amplified 3x in the algorithm.
- Target hook retention: 50%+ still watching at 3 seconds = strong signal.
- Algorithm priority signals (highest to lowest): Completion rate → Rewatches → Shares → Comments → Likes.

#### YouTube
- **5-second rule:** Pre-roll ads are skippable after 5 seconds. Hook must deliver enough value or curiosity to prevent the skip.
- In-stream: 5 seconds before skip available. First 5 seconds = the entire game.
- Mid-roll: 30-second minimum before skip (lower-engagement context; viewer is already in passive mode).
- In-feed (discovery): thumbnail + first frame + headline determine click. No auto-play hook.
- Retention cliff at 30% for long-form YouTube pre-roll (industry benchmark).

#### Google Display / Discovery
- Banner blindness is real: users have developed visual patterns to ignore display ads.
- Disruption requires: unexpected placement, strong color contrast with surrounding content, or compelling offer that intercepts a browsing decision.
- Native display (Discovery campaigns) performs significantly better than standard display; follows editorial content pattern.

### Scroll-Stopping Rate Targets
- 50%+ hook-to-3-second-view ratio = strong creative
- 30-50% = acceptable, optimize hook
- Below 30% = kill or completely rebuild hook; body copy irrelevant if hook fails

### The 85% Sound-Off Rule (Meta)
- Original source: Facebook/Meta internal data, widely reported across ad industry
- Practical implication: every ad must tell its full story with text overlays
- Text overlay placement: top-left or center-top (matches natural reading pattern)
- Captions on talking-head video: mandatory; increase watch time 12% (Meta data)

## 9. Creative Testing Framework

### Creative Testing Hierarchy (Adam Kropáček framework)
Test in this order — impact on outcome decreases down the list:
1. Concept / angle (biggest impact on results)
2. Hook / headline (second biggest)
3. Visual style / format
4. Body copy
5. CTA (smallest marginal impact)

Rule: Never test body copy variations when the angle is unproven. Angle must win first.

### 70/30 Testing Budget Rule
- 70% of budget → proven angles/campaigns
- 30% of budget → test batches
- Testing window: 2-4 weeks per batch before drawing conclusions

### Batch Generation Framework (3-Wave System — Adam Kropáček)
1. **Wave 1 — Core angles:** 3-5 distinct angles, 5 ad variations per angle (15-25 ads). Each angle addresses a different motivation driver.
2. **Wave 2 — Expand winners:** After 7-14 days, identify top 20% by primary metric. Create 10 more variations from winning angles — different hooks, same concept.
3. **Wave 3 — Wild cards:** Test 2-3 completely different angles (contrarian, emotional, format shift). Some will fail; one may become the new control.

### Kill Rules
- Impression threshold before kill: 500+ impressions minimum for CTR signal; 50+ conversions for CPA signal
- Kill on: CTR < 0.8% on Meta (cold), CPC > 2x benchmark, 7-day ROAS < 1.5x
- Never kill: within first 48-72 hours (learning phase data is noisy)
- Scaling rule: maximum 20-30% budget increase per day; larger increases reset the learning algorithm

### A/B Test Statistical Standards
- Minimum sample: 500 impressions per variant for CTR
- Minimum conversions: 50 per variant for CPA/CVR confidence
- Statistical significance target: 95% confidence
- Test one variable per variant — angle, hook, visual, CTA; never two simultaneously
- Control + 4 challengers = optimal test structure per cycle

## 10. Video Ad Structure

### Standard 15-30 Second E-commerce Ad (Adam Kropáček framework)
- **0-3 sec:** Hook — pattern interrupt, question, or bold statement. Text overlay mandatory (85% sound-off).
- **3-8 sec:** Problem — relatable pain point. Make it specific. "You've spent €10K and got 1.2x ROAS" not "ads aren't working."
- **8-20 sec:** Solution — product/benefit demo. Show, don't tell. Concrete outcomes.
- **20-30 sec:** CTA — one clear next step. No options. "Shop now," "Get yours," "Apply."

### Brand Story Video (Adam Kropáček / Brunson framework)
- **60-120 seconds** for full brand film; 15-30 seconds for social short
- Structure: Character established (5s) → Problem introduced (10s) → Tension built (20s) → Discovery moment (10s) → Resolution shown (20s) → Brand position revealed (15s) → CTA (5s)
- Guiding principle: Position audience as hero, brand as mentor (Brunson — Expert Secrets, Chapter 8)
- Show, don't tell — let visuals reveal values, not taglines

### UGC / Direct-to-Camera Hook Pattern (TikTok + Meta)
- Speaker on camera, speaking directly to viewer, informal setting
- "I need to tell you something..." opener creates intimacy
- First 3 seconds: unusual visual framing (not studio background) OR surprising opening statement
- Subtitles mandatory — auto-generated captions increase completion 12%

## 11. Funnel × Awareness Level Matching

### Eugene Schwartz — 5 Awareness Levels (Breakthrough Advertising, 1966)
| Level | State | What they know | Message type |
|-------|-------|----------------|--------------|
| 1 — Unaware | No problem awareness | Nothing about their problem | Story, shock, vivid imagery |
| 2 — Problem-aware | Know the problem | Their problem, no solutions | Problem amplification + solution exist |
| 3 — Solution-aware | Know solutions exist | Category exists, not your brand | Your differentiation |
| 4 — Product-aware | Know your brand | You exist, not convinced | Your proof, objection handling |
| 5 — Most aware | Ready to buy | Everything | Offer, price, guarantee, CTA |

Mismatch = wasted spend: pushing a Level 5 offer (price, CTA) to a Level 1 audience fails because they don't know they have a problem yet. Cold traffic = Levels 1-2. Retarget warm = Levels 3-4. Cart abandoners = Level 5.

| Exclude recent converters | — | 7-14 days | Exclude |
| Exclude <10s bounce | — | — | Exclude |

## 14. Marketing Psychology — Mental Models (Adam — marketing-psychology)

Foundational thinking patterns applied to ad strategy:

**First Principles (Descartes / Musk application)**
Break any marketing problem to component facts; reason up from there rather than by analogy. "Everyone in our niche runs awareness campaigns" is analogy-reasoning. First principles: who knows us? Why don't they buy? What's the actual friction? Solve the actual friction.

**Jobs to Be Done (Christensen)**
People don't buy products — they hire them to accomplish a job. The job has functional, social, and emotional dimensions. Ad copy that addresses the job-to-be-done outperforms feature-list copy because it speaks to motivation, not specification.
Application: interview buyers ("what were you trying to accomplish when you hired this product?"); use their exact language in ad copy.

**Inversion (Charlie Munger)**
Instead of asking "how do we make great ads?", ask "what makes ads fail?" — then eliminate those. Prevents assumption-following and forces constraint-based thinking.
Ad inversion checklist: What's the weakest hook point? Where does the funnel lose most people? What would cause this campaign to fail by month 2?

**Systems Thinking (Forrester/Senge)**
Ads operate in feedback loops. A campaign that works creates demand → demand creates revenue → revenue enables more budget → budget enables scale → scale increases CPMs → CPMs compress ROAS. Model the whole system, not just one node. Build for steady-state, not peak spike.

**Growth Flywheels (Bezos/Parker)**
Reinvestment compounds growth. Structure: more ad spend → more customers → more reviews/social proof → better conversion rate → lower effective CAC → more margin to spend on ads → cycle repeats. Design campaigns to feed the flywheel, not just drive one-time conversion.

**Viral Mechanics (Earned vs Paid)**
Earned distribution (shares, word-of-mouth) amplifies paid distribution. Identify which creative generates shares, not just clicks. Shareable content has: high surprise factor, strong identity signal (viewer shares to signal who they are), or genuine utility (viewer shares to help someone they know). Blend paid + earned in creative strategy.

## 15. Pricing Psychology — Mechanisms (Adam — marketing-psychology)

**Charm Pricing**
$29 vs $30: left-digit anchoring. The first digit dominates perception — $29 reads as "twenty-something," not "thirty." Effect size: 20-30% preference lift in controlled pricing studies. Apply to any price ending: €97, €197, €497, €2,970.

**Price Anchoring**
The first price seen sets the reference frame for all evaluation. Mechanism: Tversky & Kahneman (1974) — initial number anchors all subsequent judgment.
Application hierarchy:
1. Show total value stack before price: "Total value: €3,400 → Today: €997"
2. Show higher-tier plan first; lower-tier appears more reasonable
3. Annual plan before monthly — monthly price looks smaller against annual reference

**Decoy Effect (Asymmetric Dominance — Ariely)**
A third option (the decoy) makes one of two original options clearly superior. Classic structure:
- Small: €9/month (1 user, 5GB)
- Medium: €25/month (10 users, 50GB) ← decoy
- Large: €27/month (unlimited users, 100GB) ← target

The decoy (Medium) makes Large look like obvious value. Effect: 73-87% of users select the target option when decoy is present vs 50-50 without it (Ariely, Predictably Irrational, 2008).
Ad application: show pricing table in ads; design tiers so Medium looks bad relative to Large.

**Mental Accounting (Thaler)**
People maintain separate mental "budgets" for different spending categories. $200 "for entertainment" feels different from $200 "for software." Frame your product's cost against the right mental account.
Examples: "Less than your monthly coffee spend" (trivializes via small-category comparison), "A fraction of what an agency charges" (reframes the relevant comparison class), "3% of your expected ROAS improvement" (ROI frame).

**Payment Timing (Prelec & Loewenstein)**
Pain of payment is highest when it coincides with consumption. Subscription payment decouples pain from use — monthly feels smaller than annual lump sum even if annual is discounted. For high-ticket: installments reduce perceived pain even when total cost is higher.
