# Sales Psychology Frameworks

Practical decision frameworks for persuasion, selling, and negotiation. Compiled from Cialdini, SPIN, Challenger, Voss, Kahneman, Schwartz, Halbert, and Hormozi.

---

## 1. Cialdini — 6 Principles of Influence

### Reciprocity
- Give value without conditions → the other party feels obligation to return.
- Application: share free research, insights, or recommendations BEFORE asking for a meeting.
- Execution: free report, exclusive data, personal analysis delivered unprompted.

### Commitment and Consistency
- Small agreement → larger agreement (foot-in-the-door).
- Micro-commitments: agree on the problem → agree on the process → agree on timing → agree on purchase.
- Example sequence: "Do you agree [problem X] is costly?" → "Would you look at a one-pager?" → "Can we schedule a 20-minute call?"

### Social Proof
- "If others are doing it, it must be right."
- Use specific numbers: "174 clients, average deal size $X, 0 failures since [year]."
- Vague social proof ("hundreds of happy customers") is worth almost nothing compared to specific metrics.

### Authority
- People trust experts and institutions.
- Application: regulatory credentials, certifications, media mentions, documented track record with numbers.

### Liking
- People buy from people they like or who are similar to them.
- Application: build personal rapport before the business conversation. Authenticity over polish.

### Scarcity
- Limited availability → higher perceived value.
- Application: limited allocation, deadline-based close, maximum capacity.
- **Rule: scarcity must be real, not manufactured.** Fake scarcity destroys trust permanently.

## 2. Sales Frameworks — When to Use Which

| Situation | Framework | Why |
|-----------|-----------|-----|
| Complex B2B, long cycle | SPIN Selling | Deep discovery, urgency through implication |
| Enterprise, many stakeholders | Challenger + MEDDPICC | Commercial insight + decision mapping |
| Lead qualification | BANT (fast) / MEDDPICC (deep) | Verify budget/authority/need/timeline |
| High-ticket personal sale | SPIN + Cialdini | Build need before presenting |
| Cold outreach | Challenger (Teach) | Bring insight, not pitch |

### SPIN Selling
- **S** (Situation): "How are you currently handling [X]?" "What's your experience with [Y]?"
- **P** (Problem): "What frustrates you about [current state]?" "Where do you feel the most risk?"
- **I** (Implication): "If this stays the same, what happens in 3 years?" — BUILD URGENCY here.
- **N** (Need-Payoff): "If you had [solution X], what would that mean for you?"
- **Key rule: never jump to solution after Problem. Always Implication first, then present.**

### Challenger Sale
- **Teach**: bring a commercial insight that changes how the client thinks about their problem.
- **Tailor**: adapt messaging to each stakeholder's specific priorities.
- **Take Control**: drive the conversation; don't be led.
- Find the "Mobilizer" (Teacher/Go-Getter/Skeptic), not just the senior buyer.

### MEDDPICC Checklist
- **M**etrics → **E**conomic Buyer → **D**ecision Criteria → **D**ecision Process → **P**aper Process → **I**dentify Pain → **C**hampion → **C**ompetition

## 3. Pricing Psychology

### Anchoring
- The first number becomes the mental reference point.
- Always open with a higher value: "The project generates $X annually. Minimum engagement: $Y."
- When $Y is the real target, the anchor makes it look accessible.

### Decoy Effect (Economist Effect)
- Three options: expensive, middle (target), entry — middle wins.
- The premium tier exists primarily to make the middle tier look like the rational choice.
- Documented +42% revenue effect in studies.

### Framing
- "90% chance of success" beats "10% chance of failure" — always frame positively.
- Loss aversion is 2× stronger than equivalent gain (Kahneman): "You're losing $X per year" is more effective than "You could gain $X."

### Hormozi Value Equation
```
Value = (Dream Outcome × Probability of Success) / (Time to Result × Effort Required)
```
- Maximize: outcome + proof (track record, guarantees).
- Minimize: time to first result + client effort (simple onboarding).

### Offer Stacking
- Add bonuses that increase perceived value without increasing your cost.
- Example: "Core product + quarterly report + access to team + annual review event."
- Name each bonus and assign it a value.

### Three-Tier Pricing Structure (Good-Better-Best)
- Entry tier: maximizes number of clients.
- Core tier: natural target, should look like the obvious rational choice.
- Premium tier: exclusivity, personalized access.
- **Rule: Core tier must look like the wisest choice — not cheapest, not most expensive.**

## 4. Negotiation — Chris Voss Method

### Mirroring
- Repeat the last 2-3 words the other person said → creates feeling of being heard, encourages them to keep talking.

### Labeling
- Name the emotion: "It seems like security is the key issue for you."
- "I sense that you're most concerned about what happens if something goes wrong."

### Calibrated Questions
- Start with "How" and "What" — open, non-threatening.
- "How would you want to handle this?" "What would an ideal outcome look like?"
- Avoid "Why" — it sounds accusatory.

### No-Oriented Questions
- "No" is liberating, "yes" creates obligation → frame questions to invite "no" as the answer.
- "Would it be absurd to look at this project?" → "No, actually..."

### Tactical Empathy
- Understand their position without agreeing with it. Name it, validate it, continue.

### Late Night FM DJ Voice
- In key moments: slow down, lower your tone → projects calm and confidence.

### Accusation Audit (before key negotiations)
- List every negative thing the other party might be thinking about you or your offer.
- Address these preemptively: "You're probably thinking this sounds like every other [product/service]. I understand. Let me show you why [specific clients] think differently."
- Naming negatives defuses them before they can be used against you.

### "That's Right" Test
- Goal: get "That's right" (genuine understanding), not "You're right" (they just want out).
- Method: summarize their situation in your own words, wait for confirmation.

### Ackerman Model (numeric negotiation)
1. Set your target value.
2. Open at 65% of your target.
3. Counter-offers: 85% → 95% → 100% with a non-round number.
4. At final offer: add a non-monetary element.
5. Non-round numbers signal you've reached your real maximum.

## 5. Behavioral Economics — Kahneman

### System 1 vs System 2
- System 1: fast, emotional → actually makes the decision.
- System 2: slow, analytical → post-hoc justification.
- **Sequence: lead with System 1 (story, visual, emotion) → follow with System 2 (numbers, data).**

### Loss Aversion
- Losses hurt 2× more than equivalent gains.
- "You're losing $X per year" > "You could earn $X more."
- Frame the purchase as preventing a loss, not achieving a gain.

### Status Quo Bias
- People prefer the current state even when it's costly.
- Overcome: show the cost of inaction, not the benefits of change.

## 6. Copywriting Formulas

### AIDA (cold email, landing page, ad)
- **A**ttention: bold claim or specific number.
- **I**nterest: mechanism explanation.
- **D**esire: social proof with specifics.
- **A**ction: one clear CTA.

### PAS (email sequence, sales page)
- **P**roblem: name the pain.
- **A**gitate: quantify the cost of the problem.
- **S**olution: present the offer.

### BAB (testimonial, case study, social media)
- **B**efore: client's situation before.
- **A**fter: client's situation after.
- **B**ridge: what bridged the gap and how quickly.

### 4U (headline, subject line, caption)
- **U**seful + **U**rgent + **U**nique + **U**ltra-specific.
- Example: "8% fixed return in 24 months — allocation closes Friday 6pm (4.2M remaining of 30M)."

## 7. Schwartz Awareness Levels

| Level | State | Copy Strategy |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| 1 Unaware | Doesn't know about the problem | Storytelling, data, provocation |
| 2 Problem Aware | Knows the problem, not the solution | Validate frustration |
| 3 Solution Aware | Looking for best solution | USP, comparison |
| 4 Product Aware | Knows your product, not yet convinced | Evidence, case studies, track record |
| 5 Most Aware | Waiting for an impulse to act | Nudge: scarcity + CTA |

**Rule: never use Level 5 copy on a Level 1 audience. Always match awareness level.**

## 8. Objection Handling

### Framework: Acknowledge → Reframe → Evidence → Next Step

### Timing Objection (quantify the delay)
- "What changes between now and next quarter that makes it a better time?"
- "If you wait 90 days, you're giving up $X. Is that a risk you're willing to take?"

### General Objection Pattern
1. Validate: "That makes sense." (lowers defenses)
2. Isolate: "Is that the only thing stopping you?" (prevents goalpost-moving)
3. Reframe: "Let's look at the cost of inaction vs. the cost of moving forward." (shift perspective)

## 9. Halbert — Direct Response Principles

### Starving Crowd First
- Market selection > copy quality. A hungry market with average copy beats genius copy on a dead market.
- 40/40/20 Rule: 40% offer, 40% audience match, 20% copy itself.
- Fix the offer before fixing the copy. Verify the audience before fixing the offer.

### Specificity = Credibility
- "1,247,384 units sold" beats "over a million."
- "174 clients over 5 years" beats "hundreds of satisfied clients."
- Specific numbers are perceived as measured facts; round numbers are perceived as estimates.

### Fascination Bullets — Templates
1. **Prohibition**: "What you should NEVER do with [product] when [condition]"
2. **Simplification**: "The simple way to [achieve outcome] without [typical obstacle]"
3. **Problem-Solution Spotlight**: Question the audience says "yes" to + immediate solution
- **Rule: bullets promise outcomes, not features. Don't reveal everything — leave them wanting more.**

### Story → Facts Sequence
- Story first (System 1 / emotion) → then numbers (System 2 / justification).
- Story formula: "Client X had Y situation → did Z → now has W result."
- Transition: "Here are the numbers behind it..."

### PS Line
- PS is the second most-read element after the headline.
- Use: restate offer + scarcity + social proof in one sentence.

## 10. Trust-Building — Multi-Touch Framework

### Why High-Ticket Requires More Touches
- 200K+ decisions trigger high loss aversion → 6-12 touches before commitment is normal.
- Every touch must increase trust OR demonstrate value — no empty "check-ins."

### 10-Touch Sequence (generic high-ticket)

| Touch | Type | Content | CTA |
|-------|------|---------|-----|
| 1 | Value content | Market insight relevant to their situation | None |
| 2 | Personalized insight | Analysis specific to their context | None |
| 3 | Social proof | Case study from existing client | Soft: "Worth a call?" |
| 4 | Problem agitation | Quantify cost of inaction | Request meeting |
| 5 | Discovery call | SPIN questioning, no pitch | Agree on next presentation |
| 6 | Tailored solution | ROI model customized to their situation | Request feedback |
| 7 | Objection follow-up | Address specific objections from call | Request decision |
| 8 | Reference call | Connect with existing client | — |
| 9 | Legal/structural Q&A | Contract, terms, structure detail | Reserve allocation |
| 10 | Closing | Real scarcity + clear next step | Commit |

### Trust Accelerators (shorten the sequence)
- Respond faster than expected (< 4 hours).
- Keep micro-promises: "I'll send it by morning" → send it by morning.
- Share negative information alongside positive — transparency > superlatives.
- Don't sell until they're buying: SPIN phase = genuine discovery, not masked pitch.
