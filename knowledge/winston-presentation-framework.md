# Patrick Winston Presentation Framework

Source: Patrick H. Winston (1943–2019), MIT Professor of AI. His lecture "How to Speak" ran every January at MIT for 40 years.
MIT OCW reference: https://learn.mit.edu/?resource=5343

---

## Core Principles

1. **Empowerment Promise** — every great talk opens by telling the audience exactly what they'll be able to do by the end that they couldn't do before. Specific, outcome-driven, time-justifying. Never open with jokes, thank-yous, or housekeeping.

2. **Cycling** — repeat the key idea 3× in different forms. At any moment, 20% of the audience is elsewhere mentally. Cycling ensures every listener catches it.

3. **Verbal Punctuation** — explicit signposting ("the second crucial idea is…") so the audience always knows where they are in the talk.

4. **Star Framework (memorability)** — a memorable idea has five elements: **S**ymbol, **S**logan, **S**urprise, **S**alient idea, **S**tory. One core idea, all five elements applied to it.

5. **VSNC Structure (persuasion)** — **V**ision, proof of **W**ork (Steps Needed to take), **C**ontributions. Vision + evidence + deliverables. Standard structure for job talks, pitches, and any talk where you need to convince.

6. **Time + Place** — 11:00 is the ideal time (audience awake and fed). A room twice-full beats a half-empty room. Keep lights on; turning them off for slides shifts attention to the deck and away from you.

7. **Slides = support, not main performance** — if you're reading the slide, either the slide is bad or the speaker is bad.

8. **End with Contributions, not "Thank you" / "Questions?"** — the final slide stays on screen during Q&A and keeps reminding the audience of the value they received.

## The 10 Slide Crimes (Audit Checklist)

| # | Crime | Fix |
|---|-------|-----|
| 1 | Too many slides | 1 slide per 2 minutes of talk. 30-min talk = max 15 slides. |
| 2 | Too many words per slide | Max 25 words per slide. "If it's on the slide, don't say it; if you say it, don't put it on the slide." |
| 3 | Font under 40pt | Header 60pt+, body 40pt+. If it doesn't fit → cut text. |
| 4 | Reading slides aloud | The slide is the visual; spoken words must differ. |
| 5 | Laser pointer usage | Distracting and signals you've lost your place. Use in-slide highlighting instead. |
| 6 | Speaker far from slides | Stand next to or in front of slides, not across the room. |
| 7 | No white space / no air | Min 30% white space per slide. |
| 8 | Background clutter and logos | No logos on every slide. No gradient backgrounds. Clean, calm, solid. |
| 9 | Collaborators list as final slide | Credit the team verbally, not on the final screen. |
| 10 | "Thank you" / "Questions?" as final slide | Replace with a Contributions slide. |

## The 6 Prompt Templates

### Prompt 1 — Start Any Presentation Right

```
Act as a presentation coach using Patrick Winston's MIT framework, where
every great talk begins with a clear empowerment promise that tells the
audience exactly what they'll be able to do by the end that they couldn't
do before. Start by asking for the presentation topic, audience, and
desired outcome, then identify the single most valuable takeaway and turn
it into a sharp, outcome-driven promise that immediately justifies the
audience's time. Build the first 60 seconds around that promise—set
context, create urgency, and explain why this matters right now—ensuring
every word earns attention and momentum. Remove anything that weakens
the opening, including jokes, thank-yous, or filler, and focus only on
what reinforces the core message. The final output should include a
compelling empowerment promise, a tight first 60 seconds, clear cuts,
and a polished opening script that hooks attention instantly.
```

### Prompt 2 — Eliminate Slide Crimes

```
Act as a slide crime investigator using Patrick Winston's principles to
identify and eliminate everything that makes presentations dull,
overloaded, or forgettable. Start by reviewing or having me describe my
slides, then systematically audit them against Winston's core "slide
crimes": too many slides, excessive text, small fonts (anything under
40pt), reading slides verbatim, poor positioning, cluttered backgrounds,
lack of white space, distracting elements like logos, and weak closing
slides. For every issue found, provide a precise, actionable fix—not
just what's wrong, but exactly how to improve it. Redesign the final
slide into a strong contributions slide that reinforces key takeaways
instead of ending with "thank you" or "questions." Focus on clarity,
spacing, and impact—treat slides as support, not the main performance.
The final output should include a clear audit of issues, specific fixes
for each, a redesigned final slide, and a clean brief outlining what to
keep, remove, and improve.
```

### Prompt 3 — Make Ideas Unforgettable (Star Framework)

```
Act as a personal brand architect using Patrick Winston's Star framework
—Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient Idea, and Story—to turn any idea
into something people remember and repeat. Start by asking for the core
idea, target audience, and the single thing they should retain, then
translate the idea into a clear visual symbol that represents it
instantly. Craft a short, repeatable slogan that becomes the mental
shortcut for the idea, and identify a genuine surprise that challenges
assumptions and forces attention. Refine the message down to one
dominant, salient idea—no competing points—then build a story that
explains how it works, why it matters, and the journey behind it in a
way that feels both specific and widely relatable. The final output
should present a cohesive set of elements—symbol, slogan, surprise,
salient idea, and story—working together to make the idea stick long
after the presentation ends.
```

### Prompt 4 — Structure Any Talk That Persuades (VSNC)

```
Act as a persuasion architect using Patrick Winston's job talk framework
—vision, proof of work, and contributions—to design presentations that
convince and convert. Start by asking for the presentation goal,
audience, and desired action, then craft a clear vision statement that
defines a problem people care about and introduces your distinct
approach within the first five minutes. Build credibility with concrete
proof of work—specific steps, actions, or results that demonstrate real
progress, not vague claims—and structure the opening so it quickly
establishes both direction and trust. Close with a contributions slide
that mirrors the opening promise, reinforcing exactly what was
delivered and keeping it visible during questions. Ensure every part of
the talk either strengthens the vision or proves the work, eliminating
anything that doesn't move the audience closer to belief and action.
The final output should include a sharp vision statement, clear proof
of work, a focused 5-minute opening, a strong contributions close, and
a cohesive talk structure that ties it all together.
```

### Prompt 5 — Use Props and Stories to Teach Anything

```
Act as a teaching design specialist using Patrick Winston's prop and
storytelling principles to turn complex ideas into clear, tangible
experiences. Start by asking for the concept and audience, then isolate
the single point of confusion that blocks understanding. Design a
physical prop or live demonstration that makes this confusion disappear
instantly—something the audience can see, relate to, and mentally
"hold." Build a story around it with real tension, a clear turning
point, and a satisfying resolution that locks in the lesson. Write a
tight verbal script that directs attention—what to look at, what to
notice, and why it matters—so the audience moves from confusion to
clarity step by step. Ensure the demonstration still teaches even if it
fails, reinforcing the concept through contrast. The final output
should include the confusing concept, prop design, story arc, verbal
script, and a clear teaching sequence that makes the idea simple,
concrete, and unforgettable.
```

### Prompt 6 — End Any Presentation Powerfully

```
Act as a presentation closing specialist using Patrick Winston's
framework to deliver endings that reinforce your message and leave a
lasting impression. Start by asking for the presentation topic and the
single key takeaway, then design a sharp contributions slide that
clearly summarizes what was delivered and deserves to remain on screen
through the Q&A. Craft closing words that salute the audience—either
through a call to action, a reflective line, or a strong final
statement—so they leave with clarity and purpose, not dismissal.
Eliminate weak endings like "thank you," question slides, or filler,
and focus the final 60 seconds on aligning the close with the opening
promise to create a full-circle moment. The final output should include
a precise contributions slide, impactful closing words, clear elements
to avoid, and a tightly structured final 60-second script that ends
the presentation with authority.
```

## Application Pattern: Investor / Client Pitch (Generic)

**Empowerment Promise template:**
> "In [X minutes] you'll know [specific outcome they get from this] — no promises, just [evidence type] and [time period of data]."

**VSNC for a pitch:**
- **Vision (slide 2):** Define the market problem in one sentence. State the scale. State why current solutions fail.
- **Proof of Work (slides 3-7):** Specific numbers, milestones, track record. No vague claims.
- **Contributions slide (final, hold during Q&A):** Key metrics + what they receive + next step.

**Star Framework for a core idea:**
- **Symbol:** One visual that represents it instantly.
- **Slogan:** A short, repeatable phrase that becomes the mental shortcut.
- **Surprise:** A fact that challenges assumptions.
- **Salient Idea:** One dominant point, no competing points.
- **Story:** How it works, why it matters, the journey — specific enough to be credible, general enough to be relatable.
